Knowledge is typically documented using tools like Power Point, Google Slides, Electronic Lab Notebooks or with the publishing of papers. Knowledge is therefore mostly shared in an unstructured format, making it difficult for humans and computers to consume, understand quickly and integrate with prior knowledge. Lifelike addresses this problem with machine learning, natural language processing and knowledge graphs.

Users can more quickly understand unstructured text, extract key information in a visual format and cross-connect it with other sources of knowledge. With the integration of many public research databases and articles, users can leverage prior work and increase the return on the investment. With the integration of internal databases, Lifelike can connect derived knowledge back to the originating data that resides in various applications such ELN/LIMS and other systems.

Lifelike stores knowledge in a human and machine-readable format enabling researchers and computers to work together in understanding and solving complex problems. Researchers can ask questions, generate new hypotheses, contextualize experimental data, close knowledge gaps and share knowledge in a structured format. With all of that, we hope that Lifelike can facilitate a long-term transition from publishing knowledge as unstructured text to publishing structured knowledge graphs, enabling interoperability.
